## The Problem with Ecological Data

Most data about the Salish Sea is held in silos: DFO spreadsheets, university datasets, NGO reports, Indigenous knowledge kept intentionally off the record. When we try to understand the ecosystem, we're reading a book where most of the pages are in different libraries, many are missing, and the ones we have were written from a single, colonial vantage point.

The DFO herring baseline is the clearest example. The 1953 population figure used as the "historical baseline" was actually a terrible herring year — a moment when the fishery had already been significantly depleted. Setting that as the floor means we're managing toward a false normal. Indigenous knowledge from Heiltsuk Nation documents what Fulford Harbour actually looked like before industrial fishing: herring balls dense enough to turn the water white. That memory exists. It just isn't in the spreadsheet.

What we do have — what Prav keeps pointing to, and rightly so — is **salmon data**. DFO has tracked Pacific salmon returns by river system, species, and year for decades. It's the most detailed, longitudinal ecological dataset we have for this bioregion. And it tells an unmistakable story: the runs have been collapsing. Chinook in particular — the largest salmon, the primary prey of Southern Resident orca — are a fraction of their historical numbers.

Salmon data is our leverage point. It's the thread we can pull on to reveal the whole web.

The Salish Sea Dreaming has the opportunity to make this visible — not as a data visualization lecture, but as felt experience. What does it mean to stand in a space where the water is dreaming what it used to be?

## The Cascade: Herring → Salmon → Orca

The ecological story of the Salish Sea is not three separate crises. It's one cascade, and salmon are in the middle of it.

**Herring are the foundation.** Forage fish are what the food web runs on. When herring populations collapse, the reverberations travel upward. Juvenile salmon lose their primary food source in the critical early months of their ocean entry. Chinook growth rates fall. Fewer fish reach maturity. Fewer return.

**Salmon are the bridge.** Every Chinook that doesn't return is a meal that doesn't reach the Southern Resident orca — the J, K, and L pods that are the most visible, most culturally legible, most heartbreaking emblem of the Salish Sea's condition. NOAA and DFO have both documented that prey availability is the primary driver of Southern Resident orca population decline. The pods are starving. We can trace that starvation back through the cascade to the herring.

**Orca are the indicator.** They're also the mascots. The Southern Residents are named individuals — J35 (Tahlequah), who carried her dead calf for 17 days; J2 (Granny), who died at an estimated 105 years old. People know these orca by name. They grieve them. This is the door through which the rest of the story can enter.

The cascade also runs backward through time, into the forest. Salmon carry ocean nutrients inland in their bodies. Bears and eagles carry salmon carcasses into the trees. Salmon-derived nitrogen shows up in tree ring data hundreds of meters from streams. When salmon runs collapse, the forest feels it. The entire bioregion is connected by these migrations — and the data exists to show it.

Salmon run data lets us tell this whole story with evidence. It's the thread we can pull on to reveal the web.

## What the Knowledge Graph Actually Does

My technical contribution to this project is building the layer that connects ecological data to the dreaming.

The Knowledge Organizational Infrastructure (KOI) protocol — developed in collaboration with Regen Network — is a way for distributed AI agents to share structured knowledge about a bioregion. Think of it as a nervous system for a place. Each node in the network:

- Crawls the web for relevant content (scientific papers, news, government reports, land stewardship projects)
- Extracts structured data according to a shared ontology: people, organizations, practices, patterns, ecological observations
- Publishes findings as an event stream that other agents can subscribe to
- Abstracts "patterns" from the local to the portable: a practice from the Cowichan Valley can become a template tested in coastal Colombia

For Salish Sea Dreaming, this means the installation isn't running on static content. It has a living connection to what's actually happening in the bioregion — current salmon escapement counts, herring spawn surveys, real-time orca pod location data from Orca Network. When J-pod is documented feeding near the Fraser plume, that enters the dreaming. When a river system has an exceptionally low return, the installation can feel it.

The knowledge graph is also how we hold the tension between data sources with different epistemological standings. DFO salmon counts and Stó:lō oral knowledge about the Fraser River are both nodes in the graph. They don't have to agree — the graph can represent the tension, the gap, the contested baseline. The installation surfaces that gap aesthetically rather than resolving it politically.

## The Kwaxala Model — Worth More Swimming

The salmon cascade is also an economic argument, and this is where the Kwaxala framing becomes central.

Forage fish (herring, anchovy, smelt) are the engine of the marine food web. When they're caught, they become protein for fishmeal. When they're not caught, the ecosystem services they provide — which flow through salmon, through orca, through forest nutrient cycling, through carbon sequestration — add up to approximately **double** the harvested value. This isn't speculation: it's a growing body of ecosystem economics research.

DFO's quota system sees the herring. It doesn't see the salmon that would have eaten the herring. It doesn't see the orca that would have eaten the salmon. It doesn't see the forest that would have fed on the carcasses. The market has no mechanism for these values — which means we're systematically stripping out ecological function while appearing to "manage sustainably."

The Kwaxala model ("worth more swimming," analogous to "worth more standing" for old-growth forests) proposes a financing mechanism: account for ecosystem services, quantify trophic cascade value, and create instruments that allow communities to buy out catch rights at full ecological value. This isn't idealism — it's what the Heiltsuk and Wuikinuxv Nations have been arguing in court. They challenged DFO on data sovereignty and won control of their own herring stock assessments. They did this because the official science was ignoring what they knew from centuries of observation.

The salmon data is what makes the economic argument legible. We can show the correlation between herring decline and Chinook decline. We can show the Chinook decline and the orca starvation data. The cascade is documentable. The Kwaxala model names what should be paid for. The salmon data shows what isn't being accounted for.

For the installation: the dreaming knows the cascade even when the visitor doesn't. The Kwaxala model is embedded in how the system values what it sees.

## How the Layers Connect

The three-layer architecture emerged from Brad, Shawn, and my different contributions, but they map onto each other in a way that feels structurally sound — and salmon is a good example to think through:

**Brad's agentic world state engine** generates the narrative layer — characters with psychology, memory, and will navigating a shared world. The characters can be grounded in the actual inhabitants of the cascade: a Chinook returning to its natal stream, an orca elder who remembers abundance, a herring school navigating the open water during a spawn. They have internal dialogue. They respond to the world state.

**My knowledge graph** is the world state that feeds them. Real salmon escapement data from the Fraser becomes the world the returning Chinook navigates — is the run strong or weak this year? Are the orca waiting at the river mouth? Is the water warm? The knowledge graph ensures the dreaming isn't generic environmental art — it's this place, this run, in its actual condition this season.

**Shawn's living memory substrate** ensures the dreaming accumulates. Each audience interaction gets encoded as a memory that either fades or persists based on attention. A visitor who spends time with the J-pod orca contributes to sustaining that thread. A species no one attends to gradually dims. This mirrors how oral traditions actually work — and it mirrors the salmon cycle itself: abundance that isn't tended to doesn't return.

The installation is coherent because each layer needs the others. The agents need something to navigate. The knowledge graph needs something to give it voice. The memory substrate needs something to remember.

## Why This Is My Work

I've spent the last several years building infrastructure for bioregional intelligence — tools that help communities understand and steward the places they live. The KOI protocol is the technical expression of a belief that the ecological crisis is in part an information crisis: the right knowledge isn't flowing to the right places, the right people aren't connected to the data about what's actually happening in their own watersheds.

Salmon data is a gift in this context. It's longitudinal, granular, and publicly available. It tells a clear story if you're willing to follow it upstream. The salmon runs are not just a data source — they're the Salish Sea's most legible heartbeat. Coast Salish peoples have read this heartbeat for thousands of years. The question is whether the culture at large is ready to listen.

The Salish Sea Dreaming is the artistic version of that question. Not a report, not a dashboard — a dreaming. A way to help people feel what the data says before they're asked to think about it.

The installation creates the conditions for that listening.
