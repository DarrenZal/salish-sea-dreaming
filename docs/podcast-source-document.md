## Part I: The Vision — What Are We Actually Building?

### The Core Insight

Here is a simple but radical idea: the Salish Sea already has consciousness. The herring encode information about water temperature in their spawn timing. The salmon carry maps of their natal streams in their DNA. The orca exchange songs that function as survival maps — not romantic communication but actual cartographic data, echoed back and forth until the bigger map is assembled. The cedar holds centuries of climate history in its rings. The camas meadows remember which human communities tended them.

What if technology wasn't about humans observing nature from above, but about the ecosystem using tools to perceive itself?

That's the premise of *The Salish Sea Dreaming* — an immersive AI art installation opening April 10–26, 2026 at Mahon Hall on Salt Spring Island, part of the "Digital Ecologies: Bridging Nature and Technology" exhibition. But it's also the premise of something much larger: a bioregional protocol that could replicate across the planet.

The project tagline captures it: **"We are the Salish Sea, dreaming itself awake."**

### Not Spectacle — Ceremony

The creative director, Pravin Pillay, has been emphatic about what this installation is NOT. It's not a spectacle. It's not a tech demo. It's not entertainment.

"We're just not making spectacle here," Pravin says. "What I am interested in is that when people arrive, they commune. Art as medicine — sitting with certain works. The installation is not the artwork — the collaborative process of creation IS the artwork."

The installation is designed as a ceremonial space. Visitors walk into dimness — a 4-meter by 4-meter space with a 3-to-6 meter projection wall, stereo sound, dim lighting. Fish swim in watercolor style across the wall, their movements responding to real tidal data from the Strait of Georgia. Sound carries the echo of what herring populations were and what they've become. And here's the key design principle: **stillness is rewarded, not performance.** The longer you stay still, the deeper the visual layers reveal themselves. Two seconds of stillness and new colors emerge. Sustained attention unlocks bioluminescent layers that the fidgety never see.

This isn't a design gimmick. It's a philosophical commitment. In an attention economy that rewards constant stimulation, this installation says: the bioregion reveals itself to those who listen.

### Co-Dreaming: Holding Both the Aspiration and the Nightmare

One of the most important creative decisions the team made early on was that the installation must hold what they call "co-dreaming" — both the aspiration and the nightmare, together. Not purely idealistic nature footage. Not doom-scrolling environmental collapse. Both.

The Salish Sea is simultaneously breathtakingly alive and catastrophically wounded. Herring balls still turn the water white off Hornby Island — but they've vanished from Fulford Harbour since the 1910s. Orca J35 (Tahlequah) carried her dead calf for 17 days across 1,000 miles in 2018 — a grief procession that the entire world witnessed. Oil tankers are visible offshore while humpback whales breach beside them.

The installation doesn't resolve this tension. It holds it. That's what dreaming does — it processes anxiety and hope simultaneously, outside of rational frameworks.

### Three-Eyed Seeing

The team works with a framework they call "Three-Eyed Seeing":

- **Eye 1: Western science.** DFO catch data going back to 1888. Satellite imagery. Hydrophone networks recording orca calls. Water temperature sensors. Salmon escapement counts from the Pacific Salmon Commission.

- **Eye 2: Indigenous knowledge.** Oral traditions that remember when herring were so thick you could walk across their backs. The W̱SÁNEĆ people whose name — Lekwungen — means "the people of smoked herring." The Heiltsuk and Wuikinuxv nations who challenged DFO's authority and took over their own herring stock assessments. Carol Anne Hilton's Indigenomics framework, which proposes that life — not GDP — is the primary unit of value.

- **Eye 3: The land itself as knowledge-holder.** Not metaphorical. The salmon carry navigational data. The cedar holds climate records. The herring matriarchs — fish up to 20 years old — carry memory of spawn sites in their bodies. When commercial fishing targets the largest herring for their roe sacks, it's the matriarchs they kill first. The spawn-site knowledge dies with them. That's why herring don't return to historical spawning grounds. The data was destroyed.

This third eye is the radical one. It says: the ecosystem doesn't just contain data — it IS data. It IS intelligence. And the installation is a listening device, not a display.

## Part II: The Ecological Story — Why Herring Matter

### The Kwaxala Model: Worth More Swimming

There's a concept from the Heiltsuk nation called Kwaxala — roughly translated as "worth more swimming." It's the insight that forage fish like herring have double the economic value when they're NOT caught, because of the ecosystem services they provide: the trophic cascade that feeds salmon, which feeds orca, which feeds the forest through carcass nutrients, which sequesters carbon, which regulates climate.

Markets have no mechanism for these values. The DFO quota system sees only the herring in the net. It cannot see the salmon that herring would have fed, the orca that would have eaten the salmon, or the forest that would have received the carcass. It's a system that is literally blind to most of the value it manages.

### The Baseline Fraud

Here's a data science story that the installation makes visceral. When DFO established their herring management baseline in 1953, they chose what they thought was a "normal" year. But 1953 was already a terrible herring year — the population had been declining for decades under industrial harvest. Indigenous knowledge documents a vastly different historical reality: herring so abundant that their spawn turned entire bays milky white.

This is what scientists call "shifting baseline syndrome" — each generation accepts the degraded state they inherit as "normal." The DFO's 1953 baseline has been the foundation of 70 years of fisheries management. Every quota, every stock assessment, every management decision was calibrated to an already-depleted population.

The installation's sonification piece — "The Map of Silence" — makes this audible. Seventy-five years of DFO spawn data from sections across the Salish Sea are mapped to voices — one voice per geographic section. In the 1950s, you hear a full chorus. Then voices drop out, one by one. Fulford Harbour goes silent. The southern Strait goes quiet. Baynes Sound fades. By 2025, you hear mostly silence, with one remaining soloist. The silence isn't the ending — it's where the dreaming begins.

### The Herring Matriarch

One of the most poignant ecological facts the team uncovered: herring can live up to 20 years. The eldest herring are matriarchs who carry knowledge of spawn sites — they lead younger fish to the right locations at the right time. When commercial roe fisheries target the largest herring (because bigger fish have bigger roe sacks, worth more money), they're systematically killing the knowledge-holders.

This isn't metaphor. It's information theory applied to biology. The spawn-site knowledge is distributed across the population but concentrated in the matriarchs. Remove them, and the remaining population literally doesn't know where to go. Historical spawning sites go unused not because the water changed, but because the guides were killed.

Eve Marenghi, the team's data scientist, made this visceral: "Data with variability makes good source material — herring spawn crescendo across years, harvest data as a separate compositional line. Constant directional data produces boring sound." The herring story is good composition because the data has tragic shape.

## Part IV: The Technical Build — What's Actually Working

### The Three-Species Strategy

Instead of one diverse marine model, the team builds three focused StyleGAN2-ada models, each as a separate Autolume instance mixed via NDI into TouchDesigner:

| Model | Species | Training Images | Status |
|-------|---------|----------------|--------|
| **Fish** | 13 bony fish (herring, salmon, rockfish, etc.) | 378 QC'd, CC-safe | Training on TELUS H200, 37.6% complete |
| **Birds** | 10 coastal seabirds | 1,729 scraped, awaiting QC | Next after fish |
| **Whales** | 6 cetaceans (orca, humpback, gray) | 731 scraped, awaiting QC | Last (shapes hardest) |

Why separate models? Within one StyleGAN model, you get smooth latent interpolation — a herring morphs organically into a salmon because the model learned the structural relationship. Between models, you can only do pixel crossfades. So fish-to-fish transitions are smooth and dreamlike; fish-to-whale transitions are compositional cuts managed by TouchDesigner.

### The Holonic Morphing Vision

This is perhaps the most ambitious creative concept — the food web as a fractal cycle:

Each creature contains the whole. Scales of a fish become individual fish in a school. The school of herring condenses into a single salmon shape. The salmon becomes a whale. The whale breaks the surface, dissolves into a bird murmuration. The birds dive back down into a herring ball. The cycle repeats.

The technical implementation uses a boids particle system in TouchDesigner with three behavioral modes:

- **FISH_SCHOOL:** High cohesion, high alignment, fast speed. Thin disc formation with silver flash effect. Each particle textured with GAN-generated fish.
- **BIRD_MURMURATION:** Medium cohesion, altitude variation, wave-like density pulsation.
- **SINGLE_ENTITY:** Particles collapse to a single point. GAN fills the full frame — a whale, slowly drifting, breathing.

Cross-model transitions happen through parameter changes: cohesion goes to maximum, boids count drops to 1, NDI feeds crossfade, count scales back up in a new behavioral mode. The food web becomes a perpetual visual cycle.

The stretch vision — recursive instancing — layers this fractally:
- Level 0: Individual GAN fish texture
- Level 1: 200 fish textures arranged as a fish silhouette — "fish made of fish"
- Level 2: 50 Level-1 schools arranged as a whale silhouette — "whale made of schools made of fish"

Controlled by a single `zoom_level` parameter via OSC. Zoom in and the whale dissolves into schools. Zoom into a school and each fish dissolves into its own school. The holonic principle made visual.

### The Briony Filter

The LoRA trained on Briony's watercolors can be applied as a post-processing "Briony filter" on any visual output — GAN-generated fish, camera feeds, even abstract noise patterns. At strength 0.35-0.45, the watercolor aesthetic is clearly visible while preserving the underlying composition. Average latency: 0.97 seconds per frame on the RTX 3090 — about 1 fps, viable for pre-rendered pipeline.

The trigger token is "brionypenn" — a made-up word baked into the LoRA during training. Including it in any prompt activates the watercolor style.

This means the team can paint ANY visual source in Briony's voice. The bioregion dreaming in the visual language of someone who has spent decades listening to it.

### The Data Layer

An OSC (Open Sound Control) pulse server sends real-time ecological data into both TouchDesigner and Ableton simultaneously:

- `/salish/tide/level` — current tidal height
- `/salish/moon/illumination` — lunar phase
- `/salish/fraser/discharge` — Fraser River flow rate
- `/salish/herring/spawning` — spawn season intensity

Five visualization notebooks have already been built with TouchDesigner-ready JSON exports. The data doesn't just exist — it's already formatted for live performance.

### The Sonification: Map of Silence

The herring spawn sonification is structured as a two-movement sound piece:

**Movement I — "The Full Chorus" (1950s–1980s):** Every DFO section with active herring spawning gets a voice. The sonic landscape is full, complex, layered. You hear the abundance.

**Movement II — "The Map of Silence" (1980s–2025):** Voices drop out one by one as sections go extinct. The rhythm simplifies. Harmonies thin. By the end, you hear one or two voices against an enormous silence. The silence is the point. It's not absence — it's presence. The space where herring used to be is still there, waiting.

## Part V: The Bioregional Protocol — From Salt Spring to the World

### The Leap: Art Installation as Protocol Prototype

Here's the idea that takes Salish Sea Dreaming from a beautiful art show to something with planetary significance:

What if this installation is a prototype for how any bioregion could develop its own self-perception infrastructure?

The components are all transferable:
- **Species-specific GAN models** trained on local biodiversity (any bioregion's species)
- **Ecological data sonification** using local environmental monitoring
- **Style transfer** from local artists who have deep relationships with the land
- **Knowledge graph** federation that connects local observations to regional patterns
- **Commitment routing** that turns visitors' emotional responses into concrete restoration actions
- **Ceremonial design principles** that center stillness, attention, and relationship

This is what the broader Bioregional Knowledge Commons project is building: the infrastructure for this to happen everywhere.

### The Bioregional Knowledge Commons (BKC)

Behind the Salish Sea Dreaming installation is a much larger project called the Bioregional Knowledge Commons — a federated knowledge infrastructure with 2,759+ entities across 4 active bioregional nodes (Salish Sea, Greater Victoria, Cowichan Valley, Front Range Colorado).

The BKC's core thesis: **"Commitment routing is the missing infrastructure for cosmo-local coordination."** Communities already make promises — a nursery pledges seedlings, a land trust offers monitoring hours, a collective commits labor. What they lack is a way to ROUTE those promises across bioregion boundaries, verify follow-through, and settle obligations without losing the trust that makes them work.

The north-star scenario: "A nursery in Victoria pledges seedlings. An AI agent structures the commitment. A routing scorer suggests matching pools across the Salish Sea and Front Range. A steward in each bioregion curates what fits their community. A peer verifies delivery. Evidence is linked when seedlings arrive. A proof pack captures the full loop — anchored on Regen Ledger and Celo blockchain. The next time someone needs native seedlings, the knowledge graph already knows who delivers and who vouched for them. Trust compounds. Coordination scales."

### The Pattern Language

The BKC has codified eight reusable design patterns for bioregional coordination:

1. **Participation Spectrum** — multiple ways to engage, from passive observer to active steward
2. **Thin Contract** — minimal interoperability requirements (only three invariants: what is shared, who attests, who can use it and how)
3. **Consent Boundary** — opt-in boundary crossing with explicit metadata. Nothing leaves a bioregion without permission.
4. **Emergent Bridge** — allow local categories, propose mappings iteratively with human review
5. **Holonic Nest** — nested coordinators with local autonomy plus regional coherence
6. **Governance Slot** — required policy metadata without imposing universal values
7. **Local-First, Federate-Selective** — local storage with selective publication paths
8. **Situated Transfer** — share patterns with explicit context ("this worked HERE because of THESE conditions")

These patterns are technology-agnostic. They work for knowledge graphs, for art installations, for land trusts, for community currencies. They're a grammar for bioregional coordination.

### The Holonic Architecture

The scaling model is holonic — each node is both a whole and a part:

```
Individual project (restoration site, art installation, community garden)
    ↓
Landscape Group (watershed-scale coordination)
    ↓
Bioregional Coordinator (Salish Sea, Front Range, etc.)
    ↓
Meta-Regional Federation (Cascadia-wide, eventually planetary)
```

Each level is autonomous but interoperable. The Salish Sea coordinator doesn't control the Front Range node — it aggregates with consent boundaries. Information flows up only with permission. Governance decisions stay local unless explicitly shared.

This is the same principle as the orca song exchange: each pod has its own repertoire, but when pods meet, they exchange songs and echo them back until the bigger map is assembled. Ceremony as communication plus cultural binding plus survival information exchange.

### The Cosmo-Local Split

A critical insight from the BKC work: some things should travel globally and some things must stay rooted locally.

**Light things go global:** Protocols, pattern language, software, meta-protocols, ontologies, design patterns. These are the "cosmo" layer — they can be adopted and adapted anywhere.

**Heavy things stay local:** Knowledge graphs, governance decisions, trust relationships, land relationships, Indigenous knowledge (especially under OCAP principles), commitment pools. These are the "local" layer — they're meaningful only in context.

The art installation embodies this split perfectly. The PATTERN of "train a GAN on local species, apply style transfer from a local artist, sonify local ecological data, create a ceremonial space for listening" — that pattern can travel. But the CONTENT — Briony's watercolors, the herring spawn data, the W̱SÁNEĆ territorial knowledge — that stays rooted in the Salish Sea.

### The Landscape Hub Cultivator: Proof It Works

The Victoria Landscape Group is one of nine landscape groups in the Landscape Hub Cultivator program — a 12-month pilot run by Regenerate Cascadia, described as "potentially world-first at this scale." Nine groups across three eco-regions (Salish Sea, Columbia River, Willamette Valley). Approximately 35 landscape stewards, drawn from a community of ~600 members and ~150 regenerative organizations.

The program tests theoretical bioregional finance components in practice:

- **Phase 1 (Nov 2025 – Feb 2026):** "Preparing for money to flow." Each group received $1,500–3,000 and delivered a Landscape Showcase — 15-minute story decks explaining their landscape and its needs.
- **Phase 2 (March – June 2026):** In-person bioregional mapping workshops, flow funding sandbox pilots.
- **Phase 3 (July – Sept 2026):** Integration and scaling, refining the template for future cycles.

The key principle: **"Moving at the speed of trust."** Slowing down to avoid replicating extractive patterns. Building authentic relationships before scaling. This is the opposite of "move fast and break things" — it's "move carefully and grow things."

The Victoria group's presentation for the Landscape Showcase opens with a contrast: one map shows municipal boundaries (neat rectangles drawn on paper). Another shows watersheds (organic shapes carved by water over millennia). "One was drawn on paper. The other by water." Then: "The Salish Sea doesn't recognize borders. Neither do we."

### The Living Atlas Vision

One of the most exciting convergence points is the "Living Atlas" — envisioned as a semantic knowledge graph that is simultaneously human-legible and machine-legible, with layers:

1. **Governance layer** (bottom): OCAP principles, data sovereignty, consent boundaries
2. **Data integration**: Orcasound hydrophones, Ocean Networks Canada sensors, DFO salmon counts, community story interviews
3. **Knowledge graph**: KOI protocol, Murmurations profiles, ValueFlows economic ontology
4. **Interface**: Geospatial maps + force-directed graphs + tabular views
5. **Experiential layer** (top): Salish Sea Dreaming art installations

The art installation sits at the TOP of this stack — it's the layer where data becomes felt experience. The same salmon escapement count that fills a spreadsheet row becomes a visual creature whose movement speed correlates with population health. The same herring spawn data that makes a chart becomes a voice in a chorus that goes silent.

As the Victoria Landscape Group presentation puts it: "Maps and graphs make connections visible. But visibility isn't the same as feeling... The art isn't entertainment — it's the bioregion developing new organs of perception."

### BioFi: Bioregional Finance

The economic innovation underlying all of this is what the team calls BioFi — bioregional finance. The core insight, borrowed from network science:

**Extractive economics is node-centric.** Value lives in captured resources. The more you accumulate at your node, the richer you are.

**Relational economics is edge-centric.** Value lives in what flows through relationships. "Our network is our net worth." The more connected and flowing your relationships, the healthier the system.

The herring example maps perfectly: the DFO values herring at the node (tonnes harvested, dollars earned). Kwaxala values herring at the edge (trophic cascade services, ecosystem relationships, cultural significance). One framework sees a commodity. The other sees a keystone.

BioFi proposes commitment pooling as an alternative to traditional grants and markets. Communities pool their commitments (labor, materials, expertise, stewardship hours), and an AI-assisted routing system matches them to needs across the network. The proof of concept already exists: the Sarafu Network in Kenya (Grassroots Economics, on Celo blockchain) has 26,367 users, 188 active pools, 745 vouchers, 1,200+ acres restored, and 84% positive income impact.

To replicate the Salish Sea Dreaming in another bioregion, you would need:

1. **A local artist** with deep relationship to the land (the equivalent of Briony Penn)
2. **A local knowledge holder** — ideally Indigenous — who carries the long-time perspective (the equivalent of Carol Anne)

The hardest part isn't the technology. The technology is replicable. The hardest part is the relationships — the "moving at the speed of trust" that makes the art honest rather than extractive.

## Part VII: The Deeper Questions — What's Really at Stake

### Indigenous AI Sovereignty

During a recent meeting, Pravin flagged a critical concern about "Gaia AI" (an AI representation of nature): "It seems very Western idealistic when stripped of ancient wisdom and indigeneity." The problem: AI representations of Indigenous people default to stereotypes ("flowers and the sea") rather than the complex reality of Indigenous computer scientists, quantum researchers, business leaders.

The team's response was to propose an "opt-in Indigenous traditional wisdom AI" — one that actually carries the knowledge of 10,000 years of transmission, with consent and governance by the communities that hold that knowledge. Not an AI that REPRESENTS Indigenous people, but one that IS an expression of Indigenous intelligence, built and governed by Indigenous communities.

This is where OCAP principles (Ownership, Control, Access, Possession) become technical requirements, not just ethical aspirations. And it's where the TELUS Sovereign AI Factory matters: jurisdictional sovereignty means Canadian Indigenous data stays in Canadian infrastructure, governed by Canadian (and ultimately Indigenous) law.

### The Ecological Footprint Paradox

There's an unresolved tension the team acknowledges: using GPU-intensive AI to speak for ecology while that AI has its own carbon footprint. Training a StyleGAN model on TELUS H200 GPUs for days consumes significant electricity. Running inference on an RTX 3090 for 16 days of exhibition isn't free.

The team doesn't pretend this tension doesn't exist. Instead, they hold it as part of the co-dreaming — the installation acknowledges that technology is BOTH a tool for perception and a participant in the ecological crisis it depicts. The tankers are visible offshore while the whales breach. The GPUs train while the glaciers melt. Both truths, held simultaneously.

### Art as Perception Organ, Not Product

The most radical claim is that the art isn't entertainment and isn't even really "art" in the gallery sense. It's a perception organ — a new way for the bioregion to sense itself and be sensed.

This reframes everything. The installation isn't a product to be consumed but an interface to be inhabited. Its success is measured not in foot traffic but in the quality of attention it generates. Did someone stay still long enough for the bioluminescent layer to emerge? Did someone hear the herring silence and carry it home? Did someone walk out and look at the actual Salish Sea differently?

The Kumu network map for the Victoria Landscape Group includes Salish Sea Dreaming as a node — not as an "art project" but as a peer of restoration initiatives, Indigenous governance bodies, and landscape stewardship organizations. It's infrastructure, not decoration.

### What Does the Land Want?

The Victoria Landscape Group operates under a principle called "Land Leads" — the idea that the landscape has voice and agency, and humans are in service to place. Boundaries are defined by watersheds, not political lines.

This maps directly onto the installation's premise: the Salish Sea is the dreaming subject, not humans. We're not "making art about nature." The bioregion is using our hands and our technology to perceive itself. The distinction matters because it changes what "success" means: success is not human satisfaction but ecological coherence.

"One map was drawn on paper. The other by water." The entire bioregional protocol is an argument for letting water draw the map.

### Four Directions Teaching

Carol Anne shared a teaching that frames the larger vision: "This is the time all cultures bring their gifts together. You're not coming here to claim the space — you're coming here to work with us." The bioregional protocol isn't a Western technology exported globally. It's an invitation from the land itself, mediated through Indigenous governance principles, for all cultures to bring what they carry and weave it into something no single tradition could build alone.

## Appendix: What's a Bioregional Knowledge Commons?

Imagine a library. But instead of books on shelves organized by Dewey Decimal, it's knowledge about a place — organized by the place itself.

The library has sections for species (herring, salmon, orca). For practices (watershed restoration, camas tending, kelp harvesting). For people and organizations (who's doing what, where, with what resources). For commitments (who promised what, to whom, by when). For evidence (did they follow through?).

Every community can contribute to the library. But here's the critical part: each community controls what they share. The consent boundary is built into the infrastructure. A First Nations community's traditional ecological knowledge stays in their section unless they explicitly choose to share it. A landscape group's internal deliberations are private by default.

Communities in different bioregions can federate their libraries — connecting them so that patterns discovered in the Salish Sea can inform restoration work on the Great Barrier Reef, IF both communities consent. Light things travel (patterns, protocols, software). Heavy things stay rooted (knowledge, relationships, land).

The art installation is what happens when you walk into the library and instead of reading about herring decline, you HEAR it — voices going silent one by one across 75 years. Instead of a chart showing orca population decline, you SEE a whale dissolving into particles that reform as a school of herring that was never there to feed it.

That's the bioregional knowledge commons: information that lives in the body, not just the mind. Data that dreams.
